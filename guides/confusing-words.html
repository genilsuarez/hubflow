Confusing Words & Collocations

Side-by-side comparisons of the most commonly confused verb pairs, plus the collocations that naturally go together — with clear rules and memory tricks.

makevsdo
make
Create, produce, cause a result
Something new exists after the action
make a cake • make a decision • make money • make a mistake • make noise • make friends
do
Perform, complete a task or activity
Focus on the process, not a tangible result
do homework • do the dishes • do exercise • do research • do your best • do a favor
💡 Memory trick
MAKE = you can often touch/see the result. DO = it's an activity or task.

🔘 Collocations Reference

Words that naturally go together. You don't "do a mistake" — you "make a mistake." Learn the right combinations.

❌ Common mistakes
do a mistake → make a mistake
make homework → do homework
do a decision → make a decision
make exercise → do exercise
take breakfast → have breakfast
do a photo → take a photo
sayvstell
say
Report words (no person required after it)
say + (that) + clause / say + "words"
She said she was tired. • He said "hello." • What did you say? • Say it again.
tell
Inform someone (person required after it)
tell + person + (that) + clause
She told me she was tired. • Tell him the truth. • He told us a story. • Don't tell anyone.
💡 Memory trick
SAY something. TELL someone something. Fixed: tell a lie, tell a joke, tell the truth, tell the time.
bringvstake
bring
Move toward the speaker / here
Direction: → HERE (toward you or the listener)
Bring me some water. • Can you bring a friend to the party? • I'll bring dessert.
take
Move away from the speaker / there
Direction: THERE ← (away from you)
Take your umbrella (when you leave). • Take this to the office. • I took the book to school.
💡 Memory trick
BRING = come + carry (toward). TAKE = go + carry (away). Think of direction!
borrowvslend
borrow
Take temporarily (you receive)
borrow + thing + from + person
Can I borrow your pen? • I borrowed $20 from him. • She borrows books from the library.
lend
Give temporarily (you provide)
lend + thing + to + person / lend + person + thing
Can you lend me your pen? • I lent him $20. • The bank lends money.
💡 Memory trick
BORROW = receive (arrow pointing → toward you). LEND = give (arrow pointing ← away). Spanish: prestar = lend, pedir prestado = borrow.
watch / look / see
see
Perceive with eyes (passive, no effort)
I see a bird. • Did you see that? • I can't see without glasses.
look (at)
Direct eyes intentionally (active, brief)
Look at this photo! • She looked at him. • Look both ways.
watch
Observe for a period (active, sustained)
Watch TV. • Watch the game. • Watch the kids (care).
💡 Scale of attention
see (happens naturally) → look (brief intentional glance) → watch (sustained observation over time).
hearvslisten
hear
Perceive sound passively (no effort)
It just happens — like "see" for ears
I can hear music. • Did you hear that noise? • I heard the news.
listen (to)
Pay attention to sound actively
Intentional effort — like "watch" for ears
Listen to me! • She listens to podcasts. • I'm listening to music.
💡 Pattern
Same logic: see/hear = passive perception. look/listen = active attention. Note: listen TO (always with preposition).
lessvsfewer
less
Uncountable nouns
less money, less time, less sugar
I have less money than you. • She eats less sugar now.
fewer
Countable nouns (plural)
fewer people, fewer cars, fewer mistakes
There are fewer people here today. • Fewer students signed up.
💡 Memory trick
If you can count it (one car, two cars) → fewer. If you can't (money, time, sugar) → less.
mevsI
I
Subject of the sentence
The one doing the action
My friend and I went out. • He and I are best friends.
me
Object (after a verb or preposition)
The one receiving the action
She gave it to my friend and me. • Between you and me...
💡 Test it alone
Remove the other person: "Me went out" sounds wrong → use I. "It's for I" sounds wrong → use me.
whovswhom
who
Subject of the verb
Does the action
Who is coming? • The man who called earlier.
whom
Object of a verb or preposition
Receives the action
To whom should I address this? • Whom did you invite?
💡 He/him test
If you can answer with he → use who. If you can answer with him → use whom. (he called → who; I saw him → whom)
isvsare
is
Singular subjects — and some tricky ones
news, everybody, money amounts
The news is worrying. • Everybody is welcome. • Ten dollars is too much.
are
Plural subjects — and words that look singular
glasses, police, scissors
My glasses are on the table. • The police are investigating.
⚠️ Watch for false friends
News ends in -s but is singular (is). Police looks singular but is always plural (are).
there / their / they're
there
Place / existence
Put it over there. • There is a problem.
their
Possessive (belongs to them)
Their house is huge. • I saw their car.
they're
Contraction of "they are"
They're coming tonight. • They're always late.
💡 Expand the contraction
If you can replace it with "they are" and it still makes sense → they're. If it shows possession → their. Otherwise → there.
yourvsyou're
your
Possessive (belongs to you)
Is this your bag? • Don't forget your passport.
you're
Contraction of "you are"
You're going to love this. • You're right.
💡 Expand the contraction
Say "you are" instead — if it still makes sense, write you're. Otherwise, it's your.
itsvsit's
its
Possessive — no apostrophe
The dog wagged its tail. • The company changed its policy.
it's
Contraction of "it is" or "it has"
It's raining. • It's been a long day.
💡 Expand the contraction
Say "it is" or "it has" — if it still makes sense, write it's. Otherwise (possession), it's its — no possessive pronoun in English ever uses an apostrophe (his, hers, its, ours, theirs).
principalvsprinciple
principal
Noun: head of a school. Adjective: main/most important
The school principal. • The principal reason.
principle
A fundamental rule, value, or truth
Honesty is her principle. • The basic principle of physics.
💡 Memory trick
The principal is your pal (both end in -pal). A principle is a rule (both end in -le, like "rule").
complementvscompliment
complement
Something that completes or goes well with
This wine complements the meal. • The colours complement each other.
compliment
A nice thing you say (praise)
She gave me a compliment. • He paid her a compliment.
💡 Memory trick
A compliment makes you feel good (like "I like it"). Complement completes something else.
desertvsdessert
desert
A dry, arid area (noun). Or: to abandon (verb)
The Sahara Desert. • Don't desert your friends.
dessert
The sweet course after a meal
What's for dessert? • Chocolate cake for dessert.
💡 Memory trick
Dessert has 2 S's because you always want a "Second Serving"! Desert (1 S) is dry, just like the Sahara.
weathervswhether
weather
Rain, sun, wind, temperature...
The weather is lovely today. • Check the weather forecast.
whether
Introduces a choice/doubt (= "if")
I don't know whether to go. • She asked whether I was coming.
💡 Memory trick
Weather has "eat" hidden in it — think of sunny days for a picnic. Whether is about a choice, like "which."
still / already / just / yet
still
A situation continues, unchanged
Is she still working here? • He's still asleep.
already
Sooner than expected (affirmative)
I've already eaten. • You've already finished?!
just
Very recently
I've just arrived. • She's just left.
yet
Up to now (questions and negatives)
Have you finished yet? • We haven't decided yet.
💡 Quick rule
Yet = questions/negatives only. Already = affirmative, surprise. Just = a moment ago. Still = ongoing, no change.
