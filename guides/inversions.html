English Inversions

Inversion means placing the auxiliary verb before the subject — the same word order as a question, but in a statement. It adds emphasis, formality, or dramatic effect. Common at C1+ level.

⚡ Negative Adverb Inversions

When a negative or restrictive adverb starts the clause, invert subject and auxiliary.

1Never / Rarely / Seldom
Never + auxiliary + subject + verb
Never have I seen such a beautiful sunset. Rarely does she complain about anything. Seldom do we get the chance to relax.
2No sooner... than / Hardly... when
No sooner + had + subject + past participle + than...
No sooner had I arrived than it started raining. Hardly had she sat down when the phone rang.
3Not only... but also
Not only + auxiliary + subject + verb, but (subject) also...
Not only did he apologise, but he also offered to pay. Not only is she talented, but she's also hardworking.
4Under no circumstances / On no account / In no way
Negative phrase + auxiliary + subject + verb
Under no circumstances should you open this door. On no account must passengers leave their bags unattended. In no way does this reflect my opinion.
5Only after / Only when / Only then
Only + time expression + auxiliary + subject + verb
Only after reading the report did I understand the problem. Only when she spoke did I realise who she was. Only then did he appreciate what he had.
6Little / At no time / Nowhere
Negative/restrictive word + auxiliary + subject + verb
Little did they know what awaited them. At no time were the children in danger. Nowhere will you find a better deal.

🔁 Conditional Inversions (no "if")

Drop "if" and invert to make conditionals more formal.

7Had + subject (= If subject had)
Had + subject + past participle, subject + would have...
Had I known, I would have helped. (= If I had known) Had she arrived earlier, she would have caught the train.
8Were + subject (= If subject were)
Were + subject + to-infinitive / adjective, subject + would...
Were I you, I'd accept the offer. (= If I were you) Were she to resign, the team would collapse.
9Should + subject (= If subject should)
Should + subject + verb, imperative/result clause
Should you need anything, don't hesitate to ask. (= If you should need) Should the weather improve, we'll go out.

🎭 Concessive Inversions

10Adjective + as/though + subject + verb
Adjective/Adverb + as + subject + linking verb
Young as he is, he's very mature. (= Although he's young) Hard as she tried, she couldn't solve it. Much as I like him, I disagree with his methods.

📍 Place/Direction Inversions (literary)

11Adverb of place + verb + subject
Here/There/Direction + main verb + subject (full noun only)
Here comes the bus. (NOT: Here it comes → no inversion with pronoun) Down fell the rain. On the table sat a mysterious letter.
⚠️ Common mistakes:
• Don't invert with pronouns in place inversions: "Here he comes" ✓ (NOT "Here comes he")
• Don't invert after "only if" mid-sentence: "I'll go only if you come" ✓ (inversion only when "only" starts the sentence)
• "Not only" needs "but (also)" in the second clause — don't forget it
