🔴 some vs any

Affirmative & Offers
some
An unspecified amount, used in positive statements and offers/requests.
I have some questions.
Would you like some tea?
Negative & Questions
any
Used in negatives and yes/no questions.
I don't have any money.
Are there any problems?
⚠️ Exceptions
any in affirmative = "it doesn't matter which" • some in questions = expecting "yes" (offers/requests)
Any doctor can help. (= whichever) Can I have some water? (offer)

🟢 much vs many

Countable (plural)
many
A large number of things you can count.
How many books?
Not many students came.
Uncountable
much
A large amount of something you can't count.
How much water?
I don't have much time.
💡 "a lot of" — works for both
a lot of replaces much/many in affirmative sentences (more natural in speech).
a lot of books (countable) a lot of water (uncountable) lots of fun (informal)

🟠 few vs little

Countable
few / a few
few = almost none (negative)
a few = some (positive)
Few people came. (negative)
A few friends helped. (positive)
Uncountable
little / a little
little = almost none (negative)
a little = some (positive)
There's little hope. (negative)
Add a little salt. (positive)
💡 The "a" makes it positive!
few/little (without "a") = negative feeling, almost nothing. A few / a little (with "a") = positive, enough.

🔸 enough / too

✅ enough — the right amount
enough goes AFTER adjectives but BEFORE nouns.
old enough (adj + enough) enough money (enough + noun) fast enough to win enough time to finish
❌ too — more than needed
too always goes BEFORE adjectives/adverbs. too much/many before nouns.
too expensive too many people too much noise too fast to control
